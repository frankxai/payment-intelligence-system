## Federal level: FinCEN and BSA

**Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) / Anti-Money Laundering (AML):**
- Financial institutions (including money services businesses) must maintain AML programs, file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs), and comply with Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) for transactions >$10,000

**FinCEN guidance on AI agents (evolving as of 2026-06):**
- As of 2026-06, FinCEN has not issued final rules specifically for AI agent-initiated payments
- Existing guidance: operators of systems that initiate payments are treated as the "customer" for BSA purposes; the operator's KYC/AML obligations apply to their agent's transactions
- Operators using AI agents to initiate wire transfers, ACH, or other covered transactions must maintain records of who instructed the agent, for how much, and to whom
- KYA implementations that document operator accountability satisfy the record-keeping requirement if properly designed

**Money Services Business (MSB) registration:**
- If your business provides "money transmission" services (including moving funds via agents), you may need to register as an MSB with FinCEN
- Registration threshold: $1,000+ in money transmission in a day for a single customer, or providing services to 2+ customers in a day

## State level: Money Transmission Licenses (MTLs)

**Key fact:** Money transmission is regulated state-by-state in the US. There is no single federal license. Operators typically need licenses in each state where they provide money transmission services.

**MTL requirements vary by state:**
- Some states (NY BitLicense, TX, WA) have strict crypto/stablecoin coverage
- Some states exempt small operators below threshold
- NMLS (Nationwide Multistate Licensing System) handles MSB licensing for many states
- As of 2026-06: ~49 states have some form of money transmitter licensing requirement

**For AI agent payment flows:**
- If your agent transmits funds on behalf of others, you may be a money transmitter
- If you're using a licensed PSP (Stripe, PayPal, Coinbase) as the actual transmitter, and your role is software/API integration, you may not be the money transmitter — but this depends on the specific arrangement
- Counsel routing: engage a US payments attorney to analyze whether your specific model requires MTL licensing

## CFPB and consumer protection

**Consumer Financial Protection Bureau:**
- EFTA (Electronic Fund Transfer Act) protections apply to consumer payments; AI agents initiating consumer payments must preserve error resolution and dispute rights
- CFPB has signaled interest in AI agent payment accountability; expect guidance in 2026-2027
- If your agent initiates ACH debits from consumer accounts, Regulation E applies

---

## Sales tax and 1099 reporting

**Sales tax:**
- If your agent is purchasing goods/services on behalf of a business, sales tax collection depends on the merchant's nexus in the buyer's state
- AI agents don't change the underlying sales tax rules; they change who is in the purchase loop
- Keep transaction records with merchant location and sales tax amounts for accounting (`/pay-tax-pack` output)

**1099 reporting:**
- If you're receiving payments via agents and accumulate >$600 in a year from a single payer (US threshold as of 2026-06), the payer is required to issue a 1099
- For crypto/stablecoin settlement: cost basis tracking is required; each conversion is a taxable event

## Know-Your-Customer (KYC) for agents

As of 2026-06, the US does not have an explicit "Know-Your-Agent" regulatory framework. KYC rules apply to the human/entity operator, not the agent directly. However:
- FinCEN Customer Due Diligence (CDD) rules require beneficial ownership documentation
- If an agent is making purchases for multiple end users, each end user's identity may need to be documented as a beneficial owner of the payments
- Counsel routing: required for any multi-user agent payment platform
